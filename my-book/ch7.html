The Cost of Learning

Published

July 7, 2025

In the closing years of the nineteenth century, the American psychologist  entity  [“people”,“Hermann Ebbinghaus”,“psychologist”]  undertook an unusual experiment. Frustrated by the absence of rigorous methods for studying memory, he decided to make himself both the researcher and the subject. Over several years, Ebbinghaus memorized thousands of meaningless syllables—carefully constructed sequences such as WID , ZOF and BOK —only to measure how quickly he forgot them. His work produced what is now known as the forgetting curve, one of the earliest quantitative descriptions of human learning.

Hidden within those experiments was an observation that extended well beyond psychology.

Learning is expensive.

More precisely, relearning is expensive.

A student who repeatedly studies material they have already mastered wastes time that could have been spent acquiring genuinely new knowledge. Effective education therefore depends not merely on presenting information, but on identifying what no longer needs to be taught.

For more than a decade, large language models have behaved like remarkably diligent but strangely inefficient students.

Every token in a training corpus receives essentially the same treatment. Every example contributes another gradient update, whether the model already understands the underlying concept or not. A paragraph explaining Newton’s laws may reinforce knowledge the network acquired millions of examples earlier, yet the optimization process has little choice but to process it again. Stochastic gradient descent is wonderfully general, but it possesses no explicit notion of curriculum, mastery or educational efficiency. It simply continues learning until the loss declines.

When models contained hundreds of millions of parameters, this inefficiency was acceptable.

When training corpora expanded into tens of trillions of tokens, it became impossible to ignore.

The economics are sobering. Every additional training token consumes storage bandwidth, networking bandwidth, accelerator time and electrical power. The cost is incurred whether the example teaches the model something genuinely new or merely repeats a lesson it has already internalized. From the perspective of optimization theory this redundancy is unsurprising. From the perspective of engineering economics, it increasingly resembles waste.

This realization marks another quiet shift in the history of frontier AI.

Earlier chapters argued that researchers gradually discovered waste in computation, memory movement, communication and numerical precision. The next frontier proved more subtle. The waste was no longer hidden within the hardware.

It was hidden within learning itself.

The first signs appeared in an unexpected place.

Traditional autoregressive language models are trained to predict precisely one token at a time. Given a sequence of text, the model estimates the probability distribution of the next token, computes the loss, updates its parameters, and then repeats the process billions of times. The objective is mathematically elegant and has produced extraordinary results, yet it also discards a surprising amount of information.

Imagine asking a student to complete a paragraph while grading only the very next word they write.

The student still learns.

But much of the information contained within the remainder of the sentence is simply ignored.

This observation motivated one of the more interesting ideas to emerge from recent frontier research: Multi-Token Prediction .

Rather than supervising only the immediate next token, the model is encouraged to predict several future tokens simultaneously. The architecture extracts substantially richer learning signals from exactly the same training example. No additional documents are collected. No additional GPU clusters are constructed. The model simply learns more from every token it already processes.

The improvement appears modest when described algorithmically.

Economically, it is profound.

Once again, capability increases not because additional computation has been purchased, but because existing computation has become more informative.

This philosophy reappears throughout modern reasoning models.

For many years, supervised fine-tuning occupied a privileged position within the language-model pipeline. Researchers collected carefully curated demonstrations, trained models to imitate those demonstrations, and only then applied reinforcement learning as a final stage of refinement. The procedure was effective, but expensive. High-quality supervised datasets require enormous human effort to construct, continually age as new domains emerge, and often teach the model exactly one preferred solution to a problem.

Recent systems have begun questioning this assumption.

DeepSeek’s R1 demonstrated that remarkably capable reasoning behaviour could emerge through reinforcement learning with comparatively little reliance on large supervised reasoning corpora. Rather than memorizing vast collections of human-produced chains of thought, the model learns by interacting with reward signals that favour successful reasoning trajectories. The distinction is subtle but important. Supervised learning tells the model how humans solved the problem . Reinforcement learning increasingly asks the model to discover its own effective strategies within a constrained objective.

This should not be misunderstood as the abandonment of supervised learning.

Rather, it represents another example of engineering maturity.

The question has shifted from Which learning paradigm is superior? to Which learning paradigm extracts the greatest amount of useful capability from every unit of computation and every unit of human effort?

The same logic underlies the growing importance of distillation.

Training frontier models from first principles has become an undertaking measured in millions of GPU- hours and enormous financial investment. Yet once such a model exists, its knowledge need not remain confined to its original architecture. Smaller models can learn by imitating the behavioural distributions of larger ones, acquiring capabilities that would otherwise require vastly greater computational expenditure. In educational terms, the frontier model becomes a teacher.

This changes the economics of intelligence in a fundamental way.

Knowledge itself becomes reusable.

The first model pays the full cost of discovery.

Subsequent generations increasingly pay only the cost of transmission.

Computer science has encountered this pattern before. Compilers encode decades of optimization research so that every programmer need not rediscover instruction scheduling. Numerical libraries encapsulate sophisticated algorithms behind simple interfaces. Databases preserve hard-won engineering knowledge accumulated over generations. Distillation performs a similar function for machine learning. Intelligence becomes something that can be transferred rather than repeatedly rediscovered.

One begins to notice a recurring pattern.

Sparse architectures ask whether every parameter must participate.

MLA asks whether every representation must remain fully materialized.

FP8 asks whether every bit must be preserved.

Multi-token prediction asks whether every training example can teach more.

Distillation asks whether every model must learn independently.

Different techniques.

The same question.

Where is the waste hiding?

This, perhaps more than any individual algorithm, characterizes the current phase of frontier AI research. Mature engineering disciplines eventually stop asking how to build larger systems and begin asking how to build systems that learn, remember, communicate and adapt more economically.

The language model is no longer merely an object being optimized.

The process by which it acquires intelligence has itself become an optimization problem.

Yet training, for all its expense, occurs only occasionally.

A frontier model may be trained once, distilled a handful of times and fine-tuned periodically throughout its lifetime.

Inference is different.

Inference happens every second.

Millions of users generate billions of requests, each demanding an immediate response. Every inefficiency that survives training is paid repeatedly for years afterwards. If training determines the cost of creating intelligence, inference determines the cost of living with it. And those two costs are rarely the same.
