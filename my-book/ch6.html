How Much Precision Does Intelligence Need-

Published

July 6, 2025

In 1948, Claude Shannon published a paper that permanently altered the study of communication. Its central insight was almost unsettling in its simplicity. Information, Shannon argued, could be treated independently of its meaning. A telephone network did not need to understand poetry to transmit a poem. It merely needed to preserve sufficient information for the message to be reconstructed faithfully at the other end.

This distinction transformed engineering.

Once information became measurable, it also became compressible. Engineers no longer asked only whether communication was possible; they began asking how many bits were truly necessary. Every subsequent advance in digital communication—from JPEG compression and MP3 encoding to modern video codecs—has revolved around precisely this question. Not every detail contributes equally to perception. If redundancy can be removed without altering what ultimately matters, efficiency improves dramatically.

Modern language models have arrived at an analogous moment.

For much of the history of deep learning, numerical precision was treated as a matter of scientific conservatism. Training occurred in FP32 because it was stable. Inference eventually adopted FP16 and later BF16 as hardware matured, but these changes were generally regarded as engineering optimizations rather than conceptual ones. The underlying assumption remained largely intact: more precision was inherently safer.

Yet precision, like every other computational resource, has a cost.

Every additional bit occupies memory.

Every additional bit consumes bandwidth.

Every additional bit traverses interconnects, cache hierarchies and register files.

Multiply those costs by trillions of parameters and quadrillions of arithmetic operations, and numerical representation ceases to be a mathematical abstraction. It becomes an economic decision.

The remarkable growth of frontier language models forced engineers to confront an uncomfortable possibility. Perhaps the overwhelming majority of those bits were contributing remarkably little to the final behaviour of the network.

The history of numerical computing suggests that this should not have been surprising. Scientific computing has long distinguished between accuracy and precision . Accuracy concerns whether a computation approaches the correct answer. Precision concerns how many digits are used while performing that computation. The two are related, but they are not synonymous. Carrying twenty decimal places through an equation does not necessarily produce a more accurate physical model if measurement error dominates after the third.

Deep neural networks exhibit a similar tolerance.

Gradients are inherently noisy. Activations fluctuate across enormous populations of neurons. Stochastic optimization introduces randomness at every stage of learning. The network itself is already an approximation. Demanding exact numerical representations throughout the entire computation therefore begins to resemble carrying microscopic measurement accuracy into a problem whose uncertainty is fundamentally macroscopic.

This realization unfolded gradually.

Mixed-precision training demonstrated that carefully selected portions of computation could safely operate at lower precision while preserving convergence. Automatic loss scaling addressed many of the numerical instabilities that initially discouraged such approaches. Hardware vendors responded by designing tensor cores specifically optimized for lower-precision arithmetic, dramatically increasing throughput while reducing memory traffic.

By the time NVIDIA introduced native FP8 support within the Hopper architecture, the question had changed.

The challenge was no longer whether lower precision could work.

It had become how aggressively one could exploit it.

This shift represents something deeper than another optimization.

Lower precision alters multiple bottlenecks simultaneously.

Memory capacity increases because each parameter occupies fewer bytes.

Memory bandwidth improves because fewer bytes require transport.

Cache utilization improves because more information fits within the same physical storage.

Communication across distributed systems accelerates because gradients become smaller.

Energy consumption falls because moving bits is often more expensive than manipulating them.

One architectural decision propagates through nearly every layer of the system.

Once again, the most significant savings arise not from performing faster arithmetic but from transporting less information.

Notice how familiar this argument has become.

Every chapter of this book has quietly arrived at the same destination through a different route.

Sparse experts reduced unnecessary computation.

Multi-head Latent Attention reduced unnecessary memory.

DeepEP reduced unnecessary waiting.

Lower precision reduces unnecessary representation.

Different innovations.

The same philosophy.

This convergence is not accidental.

It reflects an increasingly mature understanding of where the true costs of intelligence reside.

For years, the field behaved as though the neural network itself were the primary object of study. Increasingly, the network resembles only one component within a far larger computational ecosystem. Numerical representation, compiler optimization, runtime scheduling, communication protocols and memory layout all influence the practical economics of intelligence as profoundly as architectural decisions within the model itself.

One begins to recognize a broader historical pattern.

The early decades of computing were dominated by inventing algorithms.

The following decades were dominated by implementing those algorithms efficiently.

Artificial intelligence appears to be entering an analogous phase.

The discovery of the Transformer solved one problem.

The next generation of research is discovering how little of the original computation the Transformer actually requires.

That distinction may ultimately prove more important than the invention itself.

Consider the implications.

Suppose two laboratories possess models of comparable capability.

One requires ten megawatts to serve.

The other requires three.

One fits comfortably within commodity clusters.

The other demands specialized infrastructure.

One responds within hundreds of milliseconds.

The other within seconds.

From the perspective of users, these systems appear nearly identical.

From the perspective of economics, they inhabit entirely different worlds.

Efficiency is no longer merely an implementation detail.

It has become capability expressed in another currency.

This observation helps explain why the recent achievements of laboratories such as DeepSeek, Qwen and Moonshot AI cannot be evaluated solely through benchmark scores. Their contributions extend beyond model quality into something arguably more consequential: they have demonstrated that architectural ingenuity can substitute, at least partially, for brute computational expenditure. In doing so, they have reopened questions that many believed had already been settled.

How much computation is genuinely necessary?

How much memory is genuinely necessary?

How much communication is genuinely necessary?

And now—

How many bits are genuinely necessary?

History suggests that engineers who ask such questions rarely stop at one answer.

Each successful reduction merely reveals another hidden inefficiency waiting beneath it.

The economics of intelligence continue to unfold, layer by layer, constraint by constraint.

And perhaps the most intriguing constraint of all is not numerical.

It is pedagogical.

Must a model truly learn everything from scratch?
