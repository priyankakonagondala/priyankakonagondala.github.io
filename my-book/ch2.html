The Hidden Physics of the Transformer

Published

July 2, 2025

The most revealing problems in computer science are often not computational problems at all. They are physical ones.

This observation has appeared repeatedly throughout the history of computing, although it is easy to overlook because software has a peculiar habit of concealing the machinery beneath it. An algorithm is written as though memory were infinite, processors instantaneous, and communication free. Reality, of course, is less accommodating. Every instruction ultimately becomes the movement of electrical signals through silicon, every data structure occupies a finite location in memory, and every computation competes with countless others for access to buses, caches and interconnects. The elegance of an algorithm on paper is therefore only one half of the story. The other half is whether the underlying machine can execute it efficiently.

Large language models have reached precisely this point in their evolution.

For much of the past decade, discussion surrounding transformer architectures has been dominated by mathematics. Attention mechanisms, positional encodings, normalization strategies, activation functions and scaling laws have understandably occupied the spotlight. They are intellectually satisfying subjects because they describe how a model learns. Yet there is another, quieter discipline that determines whether those ideas can be deployed at scale: computer architecture.

It is difficult to appreciate this distinction until one considers a simple question.

What, exactly, is a GPU doing while a language model generates the next token?

The intuitive answer is that it is performing matrix multiplications. This is certainly true, but it is incomplete in much the same way that describing a modern city as “a place where people work” is true without explaining traffic, electricity, water distribution or public transport. Computation is only one activity occurring within the machine. An equally significant amount of time is spent retrieving operands from memory, transferring intermediate results between processing units, synchronizing execution across thousands of concurrent threads and waiting for data that has not yet arrived.

The larger a model becomes, the more these invisible activities begin to dominate.

This phenomenon is neither unique to artificial intelligence nor particularly new. Computer architects have spent decades confronting what has become known as the memory wall . Processor performance has historically improved at a substantially faster rate than memory bandwidth and latency. Arithmetic units capable of performing trillions of floating-point operations per second are of limited value if they must repeatedly pause while waiting for the data upon which those operations depend. A processor deprived of information resembles an assembly line deprived of components: its theoretical productivity becomes largely irrelevant.

The implications for transformer models are profound.

Consider inference rather than training. Every generated token requires the model to revisit information accumulated throughout the conversation. Hidden states from previous tokens are transformed into keys and values and stored within the key-value cache, allowing subsequent attention operations to reuse earlier computations rather than recompute them from scratch. Conceptually, this is an elegant optimization. Computationally, it introduces an entirely different problem.

The cache grows monotonically with context length.

A conversation containing a few hundred tokens presents little difficulty. A conversation spanning hundreds of thousands of tokens does. Every newly generated token now requires reading an increasingly large collection of keys and values distributed across high-bandwidth memory. The arithmetic required to compute attention may remain manageable, particularly following innovations such as FlashAttention, which dramatically improved memory access patterns during the attention computation itself. Yet before the GPU can perform those optimized computations, it must first retrieve the relevant information from memory. The cost of transportation begins to rival, and eventually exceed, the cost of calculation.

The distinction is subtle but fundamental.

Most discussions of transformer efficiency remain preoccupied with reducing the number of floating- point operations. Increasingly, however, FLOPs are not the scarce resource. Modern tensor cores are astonishingly efficient at multiplying matrices. What they cannot do is eliminate the physical distance separating computation from data. Information must still traverse the memory hierarchy, moving from high-bandwidth memory into on-chip SRAM, through shared memory and registers, before finally reaching the execution units responsible for computation. Every journey consumes time. Every journey consumes energy. Every unnecessary journey compounds across billions of tokens.

One might reasonably ask why these issues appear so suddenly. After all, transformer architectures have existed since 2017. Why did memory bandwidth emerge as the defining bottleneck only recently?

The answer lies in an irony familiar throughout the history of engineering. Success often creates the conditions under which entirely new limitations become visible.

Early transformer models were sufficiently small that arithmetic dominated execution time. There were simply too few parameters and too little context for memory movement to become overwhelming. Simultaneously, GPU vendors were delivering extraordinary improvements in computational throughput. Tensor cores, mixed-precision arithmetic and increasingly sophisticated compiler optimizations caused the cost of matrix multiplication to fall far more rapidly than the cost of moving data. The consequence was predictable, though not immediately obvious. Computation became progressively cheaper while communication remained stubbornly constrained by the physical realities of semiconductor manufacturing.

The bottleneck migrated.

A useful analogy can be found in transportation engineering. Imagine a city that responds to growing traffic congestion by continuously building faster automobiles while leaving its roads unchanged. Initially, travel times improve. Eventually, however, the limiting factor ceases to be the speed of individual vehicles and becomes the capacity of the road network itself. At that point, constructing an even faster engine contributes almost nothing to the average journey. Progress instead requires redesigning intersections, optimizing traffic flow and reducing unnecessary travel altogether.

The transformer finds itself in a remarkably similar situation.

For years, the community devoted extraordinary effort to building increasingly powerful computational engines. Larger models, faster tensor cores and more capable accelerators delivered spectacular gains. Yet these improvements gradually exposed another reality. The dominant cost was no longer performing computation but supplying computation with the information it required. Intelligence, one might say, had become inexpensive. Transporting intelligence had not.

This observation represents the first genuine departure from the scaling paradigm described in the previous chapter. If memory movement rather than arithmetic has become the principal constraint, then entirely different optimization strategies become attractive. Compressing representations becomes more valuable than accelerating computation. Locality becomes more valuable than raw throughput. Numerical precision becomes an architectural decision rather than merely a statistical one. Entire classes of innovations—many of which initially appear modest when viewed in isolation— suddenly reveal themselves as responses to a common underlying problem.

This is precisely the context in which several seemingly unrelated developments begin to converge. FlashAttention reorganizes the order in which attention is computed to minimize unnecessary transfers between high-bandwidth memory and on-chip memory. Grouped Query Attention reduces the amount of key-value state that must be stored and retrieved during inference. FP8 arithmetic reduces the volume of information flowing through the memory hierarchy. Compiler technologies fuse kernels to eliminate intermediate writes back to memory. Even distributed training strategies increasingly prioritize overlapping communication with computation, recognizing that idle hardware often reflects unavailable data rather than insufficient arithmetic capability.

None of these techniques attempts to make transformers more intelligent.

They attempt to make transformers less wasteful.

Viewed independently, each innovation appears incremental. Viewed collectively, they reveal something more significant. The center of innovation had shifted away from inventing fundamentally new learning algorithms toward understanding where the machine itself was leaking efficiency. Modern AI research was beginning to resemble high-performance computing, a discipline in which success is rarely achieved through a single dramatic breakthrough and far more often through relentless elimination of hidden overhead.

The consequences of this change extend beyond hardware utilization. Once engineers begin viewing computation as a scarce economic resource rather than an abundant one, every layer of the transformer becomes open to reconsideration. Why should every parameter participate in generating every token? Why should every representation remain stored at full precision? Why should every communication operation block useful work elsewhere in the system? Questions that once seemed peripheral suddenly become central.

It is here that one encounters perhaps the most consequential architectural idea of the modern transformer era. Surprisingly, it is also one of the oldest.

Long before anyone imagined trillion-parameter language models, researchers had proposed a deceptively simple idea: perhaps intelligence does not require every part of a network to think all of the time.

The idea was called Mixture of Experts .

For nearly three decades it remained an elegant concept waiting for the surrounding ecosystem to catch up.
