When Scaling Became Dogma

Published

July 1, 2025

The history of computing is, in many respects, the history of discovering which constraint matters next.

Every generation of engineers inherits a bottleneck that appears so fundamental it is mistaken for a law of nature. The earliest computer architects struggled against the scarcity of transistors. Then came the limits of clock frequency, followed by the realization that simply increasing clock speeds was thermodynamically unsustainable. Multi-core processors emerged not because they were intellectually more elegant, but because the industry had exhausted an easier path. Supercomputing underwent a similar transformation. As processors became increasingly capable, the limiting factor shifted away from floating-point arithmetic toward memory latency, network topology, synchronization overhead, and power consumption. The center of gravity migrated steadily downward through the stack, from algorithms to architecture, and eventually to the physical movement of information itself.

Artificial intelligence has followed a remarkably similar trajectory.

The remarkable success of transformer-based language models during the past half decade encouraged a narrative that was simultaneously compelling and dangerously incomplete. Kaplan et al.’s scaling laws, followed later by refinements from Hoffmann and colleagues, demonstrated a remarkably smooth empirical relationship between model performance, parameter count, dataset size and computational budget. The implication appeared straightforward: intelligence improved predictably as one increased the amount of computation devoted to training. The observation itself was both accurate and profoundly important. The mistake lay not in the mathematics, but in how the industry chose to interpret it.

Empirical scaling laws gradually became engineering doctrine.

This distinction is subtle but consequential. A scaling law is descriptive; it characterizes the behavior of existing systems under observed conditions. A doctrine, by contrast, prescribes how future systems ought to be built. Somewhere between GPT-3 and GPT-4, much of the industry quietly crossed that boundary. Discussions about architectural innovation became increasingly secondary to discussions about infrastructure. Research papers devoted more pages to training clusters than to learning algorithms. Conference presentations that once celebrated novel inductive biases now highlighted GPU counts, interconnect bandwidth, and total training FLOPs. Progress, it seemed, had acquired a price tag.

This was hardly irrational. Frontier models genuinely required unprecedented computational resources. Training runs consumed millions of GPU-hours, storage systems capable of streaming petabytes of data, and networking fabrics whose aggregate bandwidth rivaled that of national telecommunications infrastructure. The emergence of hyperscale AI laboratories therefore appeared inevitable. If capability scaled primarily with computation, then organizations possessing the largest datacenters enjoyed an equally inevitable strategic advantage. AI research was beginning to resemble semiconductor fabrication: technically sophisticated, certainly, but increasingly defined by capital intensity rather than conceptual innovation.

An interesting phenomenon accompanies every technological consensus. Once a community becomes convinced that it understands the primary driver of progress, alternative questions gradually cease to be asked. Few researchers seriously questioned whether every component of the transformer remained equally deserving of its computational budget. Dense feed-forward networks, quadratic attention mechanisms, optimizer dynamics, communication protocols, numerical precision, memory hierarchies and distributed execution strategies were largely treated as implementation details rather than as potential sources of transformative efficiency. The prevailing assumption was that these were engineering refinements capable of producing incremental improvements, whereas genuine leaps in capability would continue to arise primarily through larger models trained on larger datasets using larger clusters.

History offers surprisingly few examples in which this assumption survives indefinitely.

The steam engine did not transform industry merely because it produced more power; it transformed industry because engineers gradually learned to waste less of the power they already possessed. The Wright brothers did not achieve controlled flight by constructing larger wings than their contemporaries, but by questioning accepted assumptions about stability and control. Modern superscalar processors owe as much to branch prediction, speculative execution, cache hierarchies and instruction scheduling as they do to improvements in transistor density. Once a field matures, progress increasingly emerges from the elimination of inefficiencies rather than from brute-force expansion.

By 2024, the transformer ecosystem had quietly reached a similar stage of maturity.

Consider the state of a modern frontier training run. Matrix multiplication—the operation that had once dominated computational cost—was no longer the sole determinant of performance. Highly optimized tensor cores could execute floating-point operations at extraordinary throughput, yet GPUs frequently found themselves stalled while waiting for data to arrive from high-bandwidth memory. Entire clusters spent measurable fractions of their execution time synchronizing gradients across thousands of devices. Communication increasingly competed with computation for wall-clock time. Memory capacity constrained context length. Bandwidth constrained inference throughput. Numerical precision determined energy efficiency. Routing algorithms determined hardware utilization. The bottlenecks had multiplied, and they no longer resided exclusively within the mathematical formulation of the model.

In retrospect, this should not have been surprising. Computer architecture has long recognized what is sometimes described as the memory wall : improvements in arithmetic capability tend to outpace improvements in memory bandwidth, eventually leaving processors underutilized despite their theoretical computational capacity. High-performance computing has spent decades devising increasingly sophisticated methods to overlap communication with computation, reduce synchronization costs, compress data movement, and improve locality of reference. Large language models were simply beginning to encounter the same physical constraints, albeit at unprecedented scale.

This observation marks an important shift in perspective. The dominant challenge was no longer simply how to perform more computation. It had become how to perform less unnecessary computation, move less unnecessary data, synchronize less unnecessary communication, and allocate expensive hardware more intelligently. In other words, the optimization objective had migrated from maximizing capability to maximizing capability per unit of computation.

It is precisely at this point that the story of DeepSeek, Qwen, Moonshot AI, and several other Chinese research laboratories becomes interesting—not because they discovered a fundamentally different path to intelligence, but because they recognized this change in optimization objective earlier than many of their competitors. Their most significant contributions were not isolated architectural novelties.

They were systematic attempts to remove inefficiency from nearly every layer of the modern language model stack.

The internet has often framed these developments in geopolitical terms. Export controls, access to advanced GPUs, and national competition undoubtedly form part of the historical context, but they risk obscuring the more interesting engineering lesson. Constraints rarely produce innovation by themselves. They merely force engineers to examine assumptions that abundance allows others to ignore. Whether motivated by hardware availability, economic necessity, or simple intellectual curiosity, these laboratories approached the transformer not as a finished invention to be scaled indefinitely, but as a complex system whose hidden inefficiencies had yet to be fully understood.

The consequence was subtle yet profound. Instead of asking how to build larger language models, they began asking a more difficult question: where, exactly, does the computational budget disappear?

The answer, as it turns out, is almost never where one first expects. It is not hidden solely within attention matrices or parameter counts, but scattered throughout the countless interactions between algorithms, hardware, memory systems, communication protocols, and numerical representations. The frontier of modern AI had quietly migrated from statistical learning toward systems engineering.

To understand why this shift matters, one must descend another layer into the stack, beyond parameters and benchmarks, to examine a physical reality that computer architects have understood for decades but that the AI community has only recently begun to confront. Modern language models do not merely compute. They spend an astonishing amount of time transporting information. And, as with every sufficiently mature computing system before them, the cost of movement has begun to exceed the cost of thought itself.
